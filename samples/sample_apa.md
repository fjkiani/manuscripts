# The Cognitive Effects of Sleep Deprivation on Academic Performance

## Abstract

Sleep deprivation is a pervasive issue among university students, with significant implications for cognitive function and academic outcomes. This study examined the relationship between sleep duration, sleep quality, and academic performance in a sample of 342 undergraduate students. Results indicated that students sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night demonstrated significantly lower GPA scores (M = 2.87, SD = 0.54) compared to those sleeping 7–9 hours (M = 3.41, SD = 0.48), t(340) = 8.92, p < .001, d = 0.97. These findings underscore the importance of sleep hygiene interventions in university settings.

Keywords: sleep deprivation, academic performance, cognitive function, university students, GPA

## 1. Introduction

Sleep is a fundamental biological process essential for cognitive restoration, memory consolidation, and emotional regulation (Walker, 2017). Despite its critical importance, sleep deprivation has become increasingly prevalent among university students, with surveys indicating that 60–70% of students report insufficient sleep during the academic year (Lund et al., 2010; Hershner & Chervin, 2014).

The relationship between sleep and academic performance is well-established in the literature. Curcio et al. (2006) demonstrated that sleep duration positively correlates with academic achievement across multiple educational levels. More recently, Okano et al. (2019) found that sleep regularity—not just duration—predicted academic performance in a large cohort of college students.

The present study extends this literature by examining both objective sleep measures and subjective sleep quality ratings in relation to GPA, controlling for potential confounds including socioeconomic status, prior academic achievement, and mental health status.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Participants

Participants were 342 undergraduate students (M age = 20.3 years, SD = 1.8; 58% female) recruited from a large public university. Inclusion criteria required enrollment in at least 12 credit hours and no diagnosed sleep disorders. The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board (Protocol #2023-0847).

### 2.2 Measures

**Sleep Duration.** Participants wore actigraphy devices (Actiwatch 2, Philips Respironics) for 14 consecutive days to obtain objective sleep duration estimates.

**Sleep Quality.** The Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI; Buysse et al., 1989) assessed subjective sleep quality over the past month. Scores range from 0–21, with scores > 5 indicating poor sleep quality.

**Academic Performance.** Cumulative GPA was obtained from university records with participant consent.

### 2.3 Procedure

Participants completed online questionnaires at baseline and wore actigraphy devices for two weeks. GPA data were collected at the end of the semester.

### 2.4 Statistical Analysis

Data were analyzed using SPSS 28.0. Independent samples t-tests compared GPA between sleep duration groups. Multiple regression examined predictors of GPA controlling for covariates.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Sleep Duration and GPA

Students were categorized into short sleepers (< 6 hours/night; n = 89) and adequate sleepers (7–9 hours/night; n = 187). Short sleepers demonstrated significantly lower GPA (M = 2.87, SD = 0.54) than adequate sleepers (M = 3.41, SD = 0.48), t(274) = 8.92, p < .001, Cohen's d = 0.97.

### 3.2 Sleep Quality and GPA

PSQI scores negatively correlated with GPA, r(340) = −.43, p < .001. Students with poor sleep quality (PSQI > 5; n = 198) had significantly lower GPA than good sleepers (M = 3.02 vs. M = 3.52), t(340) = 9.14, p < .001.

## 4. Discussion

The present findings replicate and extend prior research demonstrating a robust relationship between sleep and academic performance (Curcio et al., 2006; Okano et al., 2019). The large effect size (d = 0.97) suggests that sleep duration is among the strongest modifiable predictors of academic outcomes.

These results have important practical implications. Universities should consider implementing sleep hygiene programs as part of student wellness initiatives. Brief behavioral interventions targeting sleep have demonstrated efficacy in improving both sleep quality and academic outcomes (Hershner & Chervin, 2014).

**Limitations.** The cross-sectional design precludes causal inference. Future longitudinal studies should examine whether improving sleep causally improves academic performance.

## 5. Conclusion

Sleep deprivation significantly impairs academic performance in university students. Institutions should prioritize sleep health as a component of student success programs.
